**The Art of Good Enough: Why You Don’t Have to Be a Saint to Be Decent**

Let’s be honest: trying to be a "good person" according to the history books is a recipe for a nervous breakdown. If you look at the heavy hitters of moral philosophy, they don’t exactly leave room for a Netflix binge or a guilt-free burger. You’re either calculating the mathematical utility of every breath you take, or you’re following rules so rigid they make a tax audit look like a pool party.

Todd May’s book, _A Decent Life: Morality for the Rest of Us_, asks a question that most of us are too afraid to voice: Is there a middle ground between being a moral monster and a saint? Can we just be… decent?

**The Impossible Trinity**

To understand why "decency" is such a relief, you have to look at the alternatives. The traditional philosophical menu offers three main flavors, and they are all incredibly hard to swallow:

1. **Consequentialism:** This is the utilitarian nightmare. It demands you maximize the good in every single situation. If selling your house and living in a tent saves three lives in a distant country, you’re practically obligated to do it. It treats you like a calculation machine, not a person.
    
2. **Deontology:** Thanks to Immanuel Kant, this school is obsessed with duty. Intentions are everything; consequences are irrelevant. You can’t lie, ever. Even if it hurts someone’s feelings. Even if it ruins your day. It’s moral absolutism that feels less like living and more like obeying a robot overlord.
    
3. **Virtue Ethics:** Aristotle’s contribution. This isn’t about what you _do_, but who you _are_. It sounds nice until you realize Aristotle wants you to train for "flourishing" like an Olympian trains for the decathlon. It requires a rigorous, lifelong gym membership for your soul.
    

May argues that these standards aren't just hard; they are fundamentally incompatible with having a life of your own. If you are constantly maximizing the good of the universe, you can’t have hobbies, deep friendships, or lazy Sundays. And if the point of morality is to allow people to live meaningful lives, it seems backward to demand you destroy your own meaning to achieve it.

**The Decency Pivot**

So, what’s the alternative? It’s not about lowering the bar to the floor; it’s about changing the game. May suggests that **decency** starts with a simple, profound recognition: _Other people have lives to live, just like you do._

You don’t have to treat everyone’s happiness as exactly equal to your own (which is impossible for anyone who isn't a saint), but you do have to factor them in. You have to navigate the world knowing you aren't the main character in a single-player game.

**Widening the Blast Radius**

May applies this "workable" morality to ever-widening circles:

- **The Neighbors:** This is common decency. Holding the door. Not letting your bad mood become everyone else’s problem. It distinguishes the decent person from the "asshole"—the person who, out of entitlement, refuses to acknowledge that other people exist as anything other than obstacles.
    
- **The Distant Strangers:** This is where it gets tricky. We can’t care for a stranger in another hemisphere the same way we care for our mother. That’s just biology. But we can practice _benevolence_. We can learn about the world. We can pick a cause. We can realize that our comfort often comes at a cost to others and try, in small ways, to balance the scales without bankrupting our joy.
    
- **The Animals:** Moral individualism suggests we should treat anything that can suffer with respect. Does this demand strict veganism? Maybe ideally. But for the "decent" person, it might mean simply acknowledging the animal's reality and making conscious, reduced-harm choices where possible. It’s about progress, not purity.
    
- **The Political Arena:** This is the current frontline. Decency here means _civility_. It means acknowledging that the person shouting at you on Twitter also has a life, a history, and a reason for being there. It doesn’t mean you have to agree with them, but it does mean you can’t treat them as garbage.
    

**The Takeaway**

Todd May isn't letting us off the hook. He’s offering a lifeline. He argues that we can be "citizens of the universe" without losing our citizenship in our own living rooms. By recognizing the complexity of others—and ourselves—we avoid the trap of moral mediocrity without burning out on the impossible climb toward sainthood.
