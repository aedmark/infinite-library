## The Hardest Pill to Swallow: You Are Making It All Up

There is a moment in _The Courage to Be Disliked_ where you want to throw the book across the room. It happens early. It happens when the Philosopher looks the Youth in the eye and tells him that trauma—as we typically understand it—doesn't exist.

Hold on. Put down the pitchforks.

The book, which is essentially a dialogue on Adlerian psychology, isn't saying bad things don't happen. It’s saying that we aren't robots programmed by our pasts. This is the shift from _etiology_ (study of causes) to _teleology_ (study of goals).

We love etiology. It’s comforting. It tells us: "You are the way you are because X happened to you ten years ago." It absolves us. But Adler argues we are driven by our _present goals_, not our past wounds.

Take the guy who won’t go outside because he has anxiety. We usually think: _The anxiety (cause) keeps him inside (effect)._ Adler flips the script. He suggests the guy _has a goal_ of not going outside (to avoid judgment, to stay safe), so he manufactures the anxiety as a tool to achieve that goal.

Or take anger. You spill coffee on your shirt because a waiter bumped you. You scream at him. You say, "I couldn't help it! I snapped!" Adler disagrees. He says you used anger. You needed to overpower the waiter, to make him listen, to assert your dominance in that split second, so you pulled "rage" out of your emotional toolbox. We know this is true because if your boss walked in right as you were screaming, you’d probably shut up instantly. If anger were truly uncontrollable, you wouldn't be able to turn it off for a superior.

We aren't fragile leaves blowing in the wind of our history. We are the ones turning on the fan.

### The Cost of Freedom

If you want to be free, you have to be okay with someone thinking you’re the absolute worst.

This is the "Courage to be Disliked." Most of us live our lives rolling downhill, gathering speed based on what other people want from us. We want to be liked. We crave recognition. But living to satisfy the expectations of others isn't life; it's a performance.

Real freedom is the ability to walk uphill. It’s the ability to act on your principles even if it means your best friend, your neighbor, or your mom gets annoyed. Note: This isn't a license to be a jerk. You shouldn't _try_ to be disliked. But you shouldn't fear it, either. If you are living your truth and someone dislikes you for it, that is the tax you pay for freedom.

### Whose Business Is This?

Adlerian psychology offers a brutal but necessary tool for navigating relationships: **Separation of Tasks**.

Here is the rule: You are responsible for your own actions. You are not responsible for how other people feel about those actions.

This sounds cold, but it’s actually the only way to stay sane. If a parent forces a child to study, they are trespassing on the child's task. The child has to learn. The parent can provide the desk and the books (that’s the parent’s task), but they cannot force the learning.

When you worry constantly about what people think of you, you are meddling in their tasks. What they think of you is _their_ problem, not yours. You can’t control it. Trying to control the uncontrollable is a recipe for neurosis. You have to draw a line in the sand. "This is my stuff. That is your stuff."

### The Life-Lie and the Courage to be Normal

We lie to ourselves constantly. We use "Life-Lies"—excuses and pretexts—to avoid facing the things that scare us. "I’d be a great novelist if I just had the time." No, you wouldn’t. You’re using "lack of time" to protect yourself from the reality that you might write something mediocre. You are keeping the _possibility_ of genius alive by never actually doing the work.

This stems from a fear of being ordinary. We are obsessed with being "special." We want to be top of the class, or if we can't do that, we want to be the worst in the class. We just want to be _notable_.

Adler asks for the "Courage to be Normal." To accept that you are just a person. Not a god, not a devil. Just a person. This doesn't mean you give up or stop trying. It means you stop trying to prove your worth through superiority.

### Happiness is a Verb

So, if we aren't chasing fame or approval, what are we doing?

We are contributing.

Happiness, according to the Philosopher, is simply "the feeling of contribution." It’s that internal sense that you are of use to someone else. It doesn't matter if they thank you (seeking praise is a trap). It doesn't matter if the world sees it. If you wash the dishes because you want to make the kitchen nice for your family, and you feel good about that contribution, you have achieved community feeling.

You don't need to save the world. You just need to be a comrade to the people in it. You need to accept yourself (warts and all), trust others (even if they might betray you), and contribute.

That’s it. Stop looking back. Stop worrying about who likes you. Start being useful.
