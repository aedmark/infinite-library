### We Have Always Been Cyborgs: A Primer on Posthumanism

**Based on the work of Peter Mahon**

Let’s be honest: "Posthumanism" sounds like the kind of word that gets thrown around at dinner parties to make people feel inadequate. It sounds cold, academic, and vaguely threatening, like we’re all about to be replaced by chrome skeletons. But if you dig into Peter Mahon’s take on it, the reality is much stickier, messier, and frankly, more human.

At its core, posthumanism isn’t some futuristic prediction. It’s a mirror. It acknowledges that we humans have never really been just "biological entities." We evolve through our stuff. The math is simple: **Humans + Tools.** And "tools" here doesn’t just mean a hammer; it means language, smartphones, pacemakers, and algorithms.

**Why the "Post"?**

You might ask, "What was wrong with regular old Humanism?" Nothing, really. But traditional Humanism—the kind we’ve relied on since the 1600s—tends to put "Man" in a special, sealed-off box, separate from animals and machines.

Posthumanism breaks the seal. It doesn't toss out humanist values like freedom or rationality; it radicalizes them. It pushes them past their expiration date to see if they still hold up in a world where a pig’s heart can beat inside a human chest. Think of it less as a breakup with humanity and more like a complex evolution.

### The Science of "You" (Is Not Just in Your Head)

This is where the rubber meets the... nope, not saying that. This is where things get weird. Techno-scientific posthumanism looks at the nuts and bolts of how we merge with our tech.

**Distributed Cognition: Your Brain is Leaking**

Here’s a test: If I took away your smartphone right now, would you feel dumber?

Most of us would feel a phantom limb sensation. We use our devices to remember phone numbers, navigate cities, and settle arguments. This is **Distributed Cognition**. Thinking doesn't just happen in the grey slush between your ears; it happens in the loop between you and your tools. The pilot _plus_ the cockpit dials equals the ability to fly.

If a chunk of your memory and problem-solving ability lives on a server farm in Oregon, can you really say your "self" stops at your skin? Philosophers like John Locke argued for "self-ownership," but that gets tricky when half of "you" is running on a proprietary operating system you don’t even own.

**Functionalism: If It Quacks...**

Then there's **Functionalism**. This theory suggests that it doesn’t matter what you’re made of (meat, silicon, wires); it matters what you _do_. If a robot processes pain signals and reacts to avoid damage, functionally, that’s pain. If a computer remembers data, that’s memory. This levels the playing field, allowing us to compare the inner lives of dogs, humans, and AIs without getting hung up on biology.

### The Hardware of Change

**Neuroprosthetics & The Body**

We are seeing biology and machinery shake hands. Neuroprosthetics allow bio-information to flow from nerves to wires and back again. We’ve seen electronic communication between human nervous systems. This shatters the idea that "human movement" requires human flesh. It’s not about dehumanizing people; it’s about realizing that "embodiment" is an open system. We can plug things into it.

**Genetics: The Pharmakon**

With tools like CRISPR-Cas, we can edit the source code of life. We could wipe out genetic diseases or engineer humans for space travel. But this is a **pharmakon**—a Greek term meaning both cure and poison. The same tech that cures a disease could be used to create a genetic elite, widening the gap between the rich and the poor until they are practically different species.

**AI: The Tool That Talks Back**

Artificial Intelligence is the tool that finally looks back at us. Unlike a hammer, AI learns, adapts, and mimics. It forces us to confront the fact that intelligence isn't a human monopoly.

There’s a tension here. On one side, you have the **Nick Bostrom** camp, warning us about the "Paperclip Maximizer"—an AI so obsessed with a trivial goal (making paperclips) that it consumes the universe to do it. On the other, you have the **Ray Kurzweil** optimists, waiting for the Singularity to upload our brains and let us live forever.

It’s "Bostrom’s Wager": We better design AI with human safety in mind now, because if the superintelligence shows up, it won’t care if we meant well.

### Sci-Fi as a Sandbox

You can’t understand posthumanism without looking at culture. Science fiction isn’t just entertainment; it’s an "imaginative lab."

Writers like Mary Shelley (_Frankenstein_) and Isaac Asimov (_I, Robot_) have been doing the heavy lifting for decades. They use **Folk Psychology**—attributing hopes and fears to non-humans—to test our empathy. When we cry for the monster or the robot, we are proving the posthumanist point: humanity isn't about biology; it's about recognition.

Philosophers like **Jacques Derrida** take this further, questioning the rigid borders we place between "animal" and "human." If an animal can suffer, or a machine can respond, the old hierarchies crumble.

### The Final Verdict: We Need "Posthumanities"

So, we are already posthuman. We are entangled. The problem is, our education system is still stuck in the 19th century.

We can’t just study Shakespeare, and we can’t just study coding. We need **Posthumanities**—an interdisciplinary mashup where you understand the metadata _and_ the metaphor. We need to be able to "read" the digital networks that shape our lives just as well as we read a novel.

We are living in a hybrid reality. It’s time we started thinking like it.
