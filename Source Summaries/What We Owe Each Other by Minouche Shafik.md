## Fixing the Floorboards: Why Our Social Contract is creaking

You know that feeling when you walk across an old porch and the wood gives a little too much under your weight? That distinct sense of "uh-oh"? That’s pretty much where we are with society right now. Between the lingering bruise of the 2008 financial crash, the polarization of politics, the climate crisis, and the massive reality check of the pandemic, the ground feels unstable.

Minouche Shafik, the Director of the London School of Economics (LSE), argues in _What We Owe Each Other_ that these wobbles are actually signs that our "social contract" has expired. And if we don't draft a new one, we’re going to fall right through.

### The Intellectual Boxing Ring

The LSE has always been a bit of a battleground for these ideas. On one side, you had the Webbs and William Beveridge, who basically drew the blueprints for the modern welfare state—the idea that a civilized country shouldn't let people starve or die of preventable diseases.1 People literally lined up to buy Beveridge's report like it was the latest Harry Potter book.

On the other side, you had Friedrich Hayek, who looked at government intervention and saw the road to serfdom.2 He championed the free market and individual liberty, influencing the Thatcher/Reagan era.3 For a while, we tried the "Third Way" (think Clinton and Blair), trying to have our cake and eat it too, but 2008 pretty much shattered that consensus.

So, what is a social contract? It’s not a literal piece of paper you sign. It’s the invisible web of expectations, norms, and rules that dictate what you owe society and what society owes you. And right now, the terms of service are outdated.

### The Veil of Ignorance (Or: The Ultimate Fair Test)

Philosophers have argued about this for centuries. Hobbes thought we needed a king to keep us from killing each other.4 Locke thought we needed to protect our stuff. But the heavy hitter here is John Rawls.

Rawls proposed a thought experiment called the "Veil of Ignorance."5 Imagine you have to design a society, but you have no idea who you will be in it. You don't know if you’ll be rich or poor, healthy or sick, a genius or average. If you don't know where you'll land, you’re going to design a system that is fair for everyone, just in case you end up at the bottom.

Shafik argues we need to get back to that mindset. The new contract needs to rest on three pillars:

1. **Security for All:** A guaranteed floor. No one falls into the basement.
    
2. **Maximum Capability:** Society should invest heavily in making you the most productive version of yourself (education, health).
    
3. **Risk Sharing:** Individuals shouldn't have to bear the burden of economic crashes or pandemics alone.
    

### The Lifecycle of the Deal

Shafik walks through how this applies from the cradle to the grave:

- **Children & Education:** Childcare isn't just a "mom problem"; it's economic infrastructure.6 If we want gender equality and a productive workforce, we can't treat raising the next generation as a hobby. Furthermore, education can't stop at age 21. In a world where robots might take our jobs, we need lifelong learning.
    
- **Work & The Gig Economy:** The old model—you work for one company for 40 years and get a pension—is dead. We have gig workers, freelancers, and precarious contracts. A new contract needs to attach security (benefits, insurance) to the _worker_, not the _job_, so you're protected even if you drive an Uber or freelance graphic design.
    
- **Health:** Here’s the hard truth: every system rations healthcare. In the US, it’s rationed by your wallet (can you afford it?). In the UK, it’s rationed by effectiveness and queue times. Shafik argues for a transparent system where we guarantee basic care and public health because prevention is infinitely cheaper than cure.
    
- **Old Age & Legacy:** We are getting older. We can't rely on families (usually women) to do all the unpaid care work for the elderly. We need to pool our risk. And regarding the next generation? We are currently leaving them a massive pile of debt and a broken climate. That’s a violation of the intergenerational contract.
    

### Who Pays?

This is usually where people start yelling. But Shafik argues it’s not just about taxing the rich into oblivion (though wealth taxes are on the table). It’s about productivity. If you invest in people—health, education, childcare—they work more, earn more, and the economy grows.7 It’s also about businesses moving from "Shareholder Supremacy" to "Stakeholder Responsibility."

Changing this is messy. It requires politics. It requires building coalitions of people who are currently getting a raw deal—young people, women, precarious workers—and demanding a rewrite.
