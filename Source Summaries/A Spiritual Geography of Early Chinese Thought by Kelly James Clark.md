## The Heavens Have Eyes: Why Ancient China Was Haunted (In a Good Way)

We have a habit of looking at the past through a very modern, very secular telescope. We see the ancient Chinese philosophers—Confucius, Mencius, Zhuangzi—and we want them to be just like us: rational, humanistic, and conveniently allergic to "superstition." We tell ourselves that sure, maybe the peasants believed in ghosts, but the _smart_ guys? They were just talking about metaphors and social contracts.

Kelly James Clark and Justin Winslett’s _A Spiritual Geography of Early Chinese Thought_ takes that telescope and smashes it.

Their work charts a new map of early China (pre-220 CE), and it is not the sterile, philosophical seminar room we might expect. It is a crowded, noisy, vibrant landscape teeming with spirits, ancestors, and High Gods who had very specific opinions on how you lived your life.

#### The "Clean Up" Job

Clark and Winslett identify a major blind spot in scholarship, something they dub the "Naturalizing Narrative." This is the academic tendency to scrub the supernatural out of the history books. It assumes that as Chinese culture matured, their "God" (_Tian_ or Heaven) turned from a person into a vague, impersonal principle—like gravity, but for morals. It assumes the afterlife was a metaphor.

The authors argue this is less about history and more about us projecting our own skepticism onto people who would have been baffled by it.

#### The High Gods: The Big Bosses

In this spiritual geography, the most prominent landmark is the High God, known variously as _Di_, _Shangdi_, or _Tian_.

If you look at the earliest records—the Shang dynasty oracle bones—_Di_ wasn’t some abstract "force." He was a ruler. He managed the weather, the harvest, and the fate of kings. Later, when the Zhou dynasty took over, _Tian_ (Heaven) stepped in as the supreme authority. But here is the kicker: _Tian_ didn’t stop being a person.

Despite what we've been told about Heaven becoming "depersonalized," the texts tell a different story. In the _Shangshu_ and _Shijing_, Heaven gets angry. It judges. It rewards virtue and punishes vice. It has a will. Even in the so-called secular texts like the _Analects_, Heaven is a moral force that _cares_. You don’t have a personal relationship with gravity; you don't worry that gravity is judging your integrity. But early Chinese thinkers absolutely worried about what Heaven thought of them.

#### The Dead Are Still in the Room

Then there is the matter of death. The old story goes that the Chinese didn't really have a robust concept of the afterlife until Buddhism arrived with its hells and reincarnations.

Clark and Winslett point to the dirt for a rebuttal. Archaeology—specifically the explosion of data from excavated tombs—shows that the afterlife was a high-stakes affair long before Buddhism showed up.

Tombs weren't just memorials; they were post-mortem luxury condos designed to function as a separate world. The dead needed stuff—food, texts, tools—because the soul (hun and po) didn’t just vanish. It went on a journey. The rituals described in books like the Liji weren’t empty performances to make the living feel better; they were necessary maintenance to keep the ancestors happy and powerful. And make no mistake: ancestors could bless you or ruin your week if neglected.

#### Confucius and the Spirits

What about Confucius? He’s often quoted as the patron saint of agnosticism because of a few lines where he dodges questions about death. But if you zoom out, that interpretation falls apart.

Confucius was a ritual expert. He lived in a world where feeding the ancestors was a daily reality. The authors suggest that when Confucius sidestepped questions about spirits, he wasn’t denying their existence; he was telling his disciples to focus on what they could control. You don't teach a kid to drive by explaining the thermodynamics of the engine. But that doesn't mean the engine isn't real.

#### The Verdict

By combining philosophy with archaeology and the cognitive science of religion, Clark and Winslett reveal a China that was deeply, inextricably spiritual. The "secular" China is a modern invention. The real map of the past is messier, spookier, and frankly, much more human.
