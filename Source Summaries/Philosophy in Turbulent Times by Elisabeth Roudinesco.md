**Thinking While the World Burns: A Field Report on French Philosophy**

You know that specific, creeping dread that hits when you scroll through the news? That feeling that we are obsessively memorializing the past while simultaneously taking a sledgehammer to the foundations of reality? Elisabeth Roudinesco calls this "turbulent times," but honestly, it feels more like a vertigo of history. It’s a landscape where feminism, socialism, and psychoanalysis are getting evicted, and giants like Freud or Marx are declared "dead" on arrival. It’s the sensation that we are just tallying up losses in a giant ledger, reducing human beings to data points.

Roudinesco identifies a "new malaise." It’s a psychologization of existence that acts as a sedative. It leads to what Michel Foucault and Gilles Deleuze famously called "little everyday fascism." Not the marching-in-the-streets kind, but the intimate kind—the desire for power and submission that slips into our hearts without us noticing, making us love the very things that dominate us.

To fight this, Roudinesco doesn't give us a pep talk. She gives us six French thinkers who refused to behave: Canguilhem, Sartre, Foucault, Althusser, Deleuze, and Derrida. They didn't agree on much, but they agreed on this: You cannot submit.

The Backbone: Georges Canguilhem

Canguilhem wasn't just a philosopher of science; he was a walking spine. He viewed psychology as a "school of submission"—a slippery slope leading straight to "police headquarters." Why? Because it treats the human spirit as a bug to be fixed or a machine to be optimized.

During WWII, Canguilhem was a hero of the Resistance. He didn't see peace as the absence of war; he saw it as a choice of destiny. He taught that "normality" isn't fitting in; true normality is the ability to break the rules to establish a new, better way of living. He was the strict teacher who demanded you think without a safety net. He looked at the history of science and saw that truth often comes from "error," from the anomalies that refuse to fit the curve.

The Existentialist: Jean-Paul Sartre

Then there's Sartre. While Canguilhem was dissecting concepts, Sartre was obsessing over the messy, nauseating reality of being alive. He wanted to be the "Freud of freedom," trying to reconcile the fact that we are radically free with the fact that we constantly lie to ourselves ("bad faith").

Sartre was the guy who missed the boat on the unconscious initially but clawed his way back. He realized you can't just declare yourself free; you have to wrestle with the history that made you. He and Lacan were like two planets in the same orbit—one screaming about freedom, the other coldly pointing out the structures we are trapped in.

The Archaeologist: Michel Foucault

Foucault took a sledgehammer to the asylum. His History of Madness wasn't about mental illness; it was about how society draws a line in the sand and shoves people across it to feel "sane." He dug through the archives of the rejected and found that what we call "science" is often just power wearing a lab coat.

He argued that the definitions of "sane" and "mad" are historical constructs, not biological truths. Naturally, the psychiatrists hated him. But Canguilhem loved him, seeing a kindred spirit who understood that enforcing "norms" is often just a polite way of enforcing control.

The Tragedy: Louis Althusser

This is where it gets dark. Althusser is the wound in this history. A brilliant Marxist who tried to use philosophy to cure the world, while suffering from a profound, crushing melancholy himself. His story is stained by the horrific tragedy of strangling his wife, Hélène Rytmann. It’s a "universe of crime" that philosophy can’t tidy up. He lived the collision of the mind and the madness he tried to theorize, proving that sometimes, the "future lasts a long time," especially when it’s haunted.

The Rhizome: Gilles Deleuze

If Foucault was the archaeologist, Deleuze was the punk rocker. He hated the "One"—the idea of a single truth or a single ego. He wanted "multiplicity." He saw the brain not as a computer, but as a factory of desiring machines.

With Anti-Oedipus, he declared war on the "Daddy-Mommy-Me" triangle of psychoanalysis. He wanted us to become "schizos" (in a philosophical sense)—nomads who refuse to be pinned down by the fascism of identity. He believed we need to create concepts that explode the status quo, not just analyze it.

The Mourner: Jacques Derrida

Finally, Derrida. The last one standing, whose work became a long goodbye to his friends. He deconstructed the very idea of presence, showing us that we are always haunted by "specters." He fought with Foucault about Descartes, but when Foucault was in trouble, Derrida was there. His philosophy wasn't just wordplay; it was a way of reckoning with loss, reminding us that justice is always "to come," and never fully here.

The Takeaway

These six weren't just thinking; they were fighting. They fought against the "biologization of the mind" and the "ideology of submission." They remind us that thinking isn't about getting an A or maximizing your productivity. It's about resistance. It's about refusing to be a "docile body." In a world that wants you to be a predictable consumer of content, their message is dangerous and necessary: Don't fit the mold. Break it.
