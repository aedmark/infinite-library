> NARRATIVE:

**The DIY Life: Why You Feel Like You’re Failing (But It’s Probably Just the System)**

Let’s start with a cheerful thought from Zygmunt Bauman, echoing Pascal: We are all absolutely losing it. Being "mad" is just part of the human manufacturing specs. Why? Because we are these weird, symbolic creatures who can imagine eternity, stuck in bodies that have an expiration date. We know we’re going to run into the ground, and that knowledge is terrifying.

So, how do we cope? We invent a "shared madness" called **Society**.

Think of society as a massive, elaborate blanket fort. It existed before you got here, and it’ll be here after you leave. By agreeing to the rules of the fort—customs, habits, routine—we distract ourselves from the "poison of absurdity" (a.k.a. death). When we share the madness, it stops looking like madness and starts looking like "culture." It gives us meaning. It makes us feel safe.

**The Great Rebranding: From Fate to Choice**

But here is where Bauman pulls the rug out. In the old days, society was a solid thing. You knew your place—maybe you didn't like it, but you had a team. If you were a factory worker and you got stiffed, you and the other workers went on strike. You had a "common cause."

Welcome to the "Individualized Society."

Now, the powers-that-be have largely clocked out. They’ve dismantled the support structures but left the risks. You are told you are "free." You are the CEO of Your Life, Inc. But this freedom comes with a nasty fine print: **"No more salvation by society."**

If you are unemployed, it’s not because the global market shifted capital to a different hemisphere; it’s because _you_ didn't have a good enough resume. If you are anxious, it’s not because the world is on fire; it’s because _you_ haven't practiced enough mindfulness. Bauman calls this finding a "biographical solution to systemic contradictions." Translation: The system is broken, but you’re the one who has to go to therapy for it.

**The Camping Trip from Hell**

Bauman uses a killer metaphor here. Society used to be a home; now, it’s a campsite.

In a campsite, you bring your own gear (your "caravan"). You pay your fee. You ignore the neighbors unless you need to borrow a match. If the campsite is dirty or the service is bad, you don’t organize a committee to fix it; you just pack up and drive to the next one. We treat our communities, our jobs, and even our relationships like this. We are "critics" of the service, not citizens of the place. We complain, but we don't build.

**The Cult of Fitness (And Not the Gym Kind)**

This constant instability changes what we value. We used to value "Health"—a standard you could reach and maintain. Now, we value "Fitness."

In this context, fitness isn't about bench-pressing; it’s about being ready for _anything_, _always_. Because the world is "liquid" and unpredictable, you can never stop preparing. You have to be flexible, adaptable, and constantly upgrading. "Health" has a finish line; "Fitness" is a treadmill that never stops. If you stop moving, you are "unfit," and in a world without a safety net, that is a death sentence.

**The Lonely Crowd**

So, we end up in what Bauman calls "Peg Communities." We are all lonely, scared individuals looking for warmth. So we hang our coats on the same peg for a few minutes—maybe around a shared grievance, a hashtag, or a trend—and we call it a community. But the moment the topic changes, everyone grabs their coat and leaves.

It’s a "mass sentence" of solitude. We are surrounded by people, yet fundamentally alone in our struggle. We have lost the _Agora_—the public square where private troubles are turned into public issues. Instead, the public square is just a place where we broadcast our private anxieties and watch celebrities live out theirs.

Bauman’s point isn’t to depress us (well, maybe a little), but to wake us up. The only way out of this lonely, "do-it-yourself" life is to realize that our personal failures are actually shared struggles. We need to stop trying to fix the world individually and start rebuilding the blanket fort together.
