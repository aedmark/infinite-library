**The Passion of the Brain: Foucault, Lacan, and the "Eros" of Thinking**

Let’s be real for a second: most people think philosophy is just dry, dusty people in tweed jackets arguing about whether a chair is actually a chair. But John Rajchman’s _Truth and Eros_ blows the dust off the library shelves. He looks at Jacques Lacan and Michel Foucault—the heavyweights of 20th-century French theory—and argues that for them, thinking wasn’t a job.1 It was an _obsession_. It was erotic.

Now, when we say "erotic" or "eros," get your mind out of the gutter (or at least, expand the gutter). We aren’t just talking about bedroom activities. We are talking about a primal, driving hunger. The ancient Greeks believed that the pursuit of truth was a passionate, desperate "lust" for understanding. Rajchman argues that Foucault and Lacan brought that heat back into the cold world of modern academia.2

### The Feature, Not the Bug: Why Is It So Hard to Read?

If you have ever tried to read Lacan, you have probably wanted to throw the book across the room. It’s dense, winding, and maddening. Foucault isn't a walk in the park, either.

Rajchman suggests this "difficulty" is deliberate. It’s a barrier to entry, sure, but it’s also a tool.

- **Lacan** operated like an ancient Zen master or a cryptic oracle. He didn’t want you to just memorize his "facts." He wanted to break your brain so you had to rebuild it. He spoke rather than wrote, treating his seminars like high-wire acts.
    
- **Foucault** saw the difficulty as a way to force a "realization." If it was easy, you’d consume it like a cheeseburger and forget it. If you have to fight for the meaning, it changes you.
    

**Clarence:** I have always said that clarity is the politeness of the philosopher, but Lacan was famously rude. He called publishing books _poubellication_ (a pun on _poubelle_, the French word for "trash can").3 He basically thought putting his words in print was throwing them in the garbage. That is a level of intellectual snobbery I almost respect.

### The Trap of "The Good"

Here is where things get morally slippery. Both thinkers had a massive beef with the traditional idea of "Ethics."

Since Aristotle, Western civilization has been obsessed with "The Good." We try to be Good Citizens, Good Neighbors, Good People. We follow the rules (Kant) or we try to maximize happiness (Bentham).

Lacan and Foucault looked at "The Good" and saw a cage.

Lacan, digging into Freud, argued that humans aren't wired for "happiness" or "harmony." We are wired for desire, and desire is messy, tragic, and often self-destructive. He wanted to move us from an "Ethics of the Good" to an "Ethics of the Real."

- **The Real:** The harsh, un-symbolized truth of our existence. It’s the trauma, the biological grit, the parts of life that don't fit into a polite society.
    
- **The Ideal:** The comforting lie that if we just follow the rules, everything will be okay.
    

Lacan believed that trying to be "whole" or "good" was a recipe for neurosis. The goal of psychoanalysis isn't to fix you so you can be a productive worker bee; it's to help you survive the catastrophe of your own desires.
### Foucault and the Art of Living

Foucault took a slightly different, but equally radical path. He stopped asking "Is this true?" in a scientific sense, and started asking, "What does it cost the subject to tell the truth?"

He was fascinated by the ancient idea of askêsis—training oneself. Not in a religious, hair-shirt way, but in an artistic way. He asked: Why do we treat a painting or a house as a work of art, but not our own lives?

He wanted us to invent ourselves. To create a style of living that wasn't dictated by the state, the church, or the psychiatrist.

**Eloise:** I love this part! It’s so empowering but also terrifying. He’s saying there is no "true self" deep down waiting to be discovered. You aren't a buried treasure; you're a canvas. You have to paint the picture yourself, and you might mess it up. That freedom is dizzying.

### A New Kind of Friendship

One of the most touching threads in this text is the concept of friendship.

- **Aristotelian Friendship:** Based on shared virtue.4 We are friends because we are both Good Guys pursuing the same Good Goal.
    
- **Foucault/Lacan Friendship:** Based on shared _difficulty_. We are friends because we are both staring into the abyss of the "Real," and it’s scary, and we are helping each other not blink.
    

It’s a "tragic" friendship. It acknowledges that we are all a bit broken, that life doesn't have a happy ending where everything is resolved, and that the state cannot save us.

### The Cost of Truth

Ultimately, Rajchman presents these two not as guys who had all the answers, but as guys who asked the most expensive questions.

Freud revolutionized ethics by showing us we are strangers to ourselves.5 Lacan and Foucault picked up that grenade and pulled the pin. They demand we look at the "intolerable"—the things in our society and ourselves that we can no longer stand—and refuse to look away.

It is an ethics of "suspicion." Not being paranoid, but being unwilling to accept "because I said so" as an answer from anyone—whether it’s a priest, a politician, or your own superego.
