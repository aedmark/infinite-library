> NARRATIVE:

Ezra Klein’s _Abundance_ drops a heavy anchor on a provocative reality: the future we crave requires us to simply make more. It sounds deceptively basic, but the central thesis strikes a nerve. America’s defining twenty-first-century headaches—from the housing shortage to the energy grid—are not inevitable destinies. They are "chosen scarcities." We are not running out of resources; we are running into walls we built ourselves. Realizing this is exhilarating because it implies we have the power to pivot. But staring down the barrel of _why_ we chose this path? That is the part that drives you mad.

This isn’t a pitch for more plastic junk or overflowing landfills. Klein posits that we have been wildly effective at drowning ourselves in cheap consumer goods while failing to secure the pillars of a dignified life: affordable shelter, clean power, reliable transit, and health infrastructure. The text is deeply suspicious of any worldview that treats scarcity as a virtue. Trying to fix the climate crisis or labor gaps by shrinking our footprint is a trap. The correction required is a return to production—building the things that actually matter. Real economic growth isn’t about fighting for a bigger slice of the same stale pie; it’s about changing the recipe entirely.

### The Architecture of "No"

Housing in our wealthiest cities is the clearest evidence of the crime. We love to declare housing a human right, yet we have engineered a reality where it is punishingly expensive and illegal to build. In places like San Francisco or New York, the cost of living has devoured the benefits of high wages. You pay a ransom just to exist. This creates a moral failure where the firefighters and teachers who serve a city cannot afford to sleep in it.

This is not the invisible hand of the market failing; it is policy working exactly as designed. After explicit racial redlining was banned, communities got creative with "character preservation" tools—like minimum lot sizes—that effectively locked the gate against anyone who wasn’t already wealthy (and usually white). While economists might see this as homeowners guarding their investment, there is a distinct political lineage here. A liberal backlash against the bulldozing excesses of the mid-century development boom birthed environmental laws like California’s CEQA. These laws were born from good intentions, but they were quickly weaponized by incumbent homeowners to freeze the world in amber. The result is a homelessness crisis that is, at its core, a math problem: we simply do not have enough homes.

The same paralysis grips clean energy. We are in a climate emergency, yet the environmental regulations designed to save the planet are frequently used to strangle wind and solar projects in the crib. The scale of what we need to build is mind-bending—imagine covering multiple states in solar panels, bringing massive facilities online every single week for decades. But our current permitting laws create a labyrinth for the very green infrastructure we are desperate to build.

### The Bureaucracy of Good Intentions

Why is picking up a shovel so hard? The construction sector has been stagnant for decades, unlike manufacturing. The text leans on Mancur Olson’s theory: in stable, rich societies, interest groups—from the Sierra Club to the local Chamber of Commerce—accumulate power like barnacles on a hull. Over time, the system gums up with lobbying, complex regulations, and endless bargaining. Public projects are forced to navigate a minefield of "veto points," where affluent neighbors are particularly skilled at organizing to kill development.

The incentives are all wrong. A culture that builds rewards engineers. A culture stuck in a "negotiations phase" rewards the people who manage complexity—lawyers and consultants.

Klein doesn't let the Left off the hook, diagnosing a specific modern pathology: "procedure fetishism." In a noble attempt to make government fair, we became obsessed with the legal process rather than the actual outcome. We buried progress under court challenges and environmental reviews. This is "Everything-Bagel Liberalism"—the urge to weigh down a single project (like a semiconductor plant) with every worthy social goal imaginable, from equity strategies to childcare mandates. It is well-meaning, but it risks collapsing the project under the weight of its own requirements.

### The Innovation Bottleneck

The rot isn't just in our cities; it’s in our labs. Despite throwing money at the problem, scientific progress is dragging. Part of this is just the difficulty of modern science—the low-hanging fruit is gone. But there is also the "Karikó Problem," named for Katalin Karikó, whose revolutionary mRNA research was ignored by the establishment for years.

Institutions like the NIH have become risk-averse, favoring proposals that look like sure bets over wild, high-reward gambles. We are rewarding "grantsmanship," not genius. This fear of novelty is devastating because true breakthroughs—like the drugs derived from Gila monster venom—often come from "weird trees" and obsessive outliers. We tried to cure the system with paperwork and accountability, but we ended up stifling the work itself.

### The Way Out

So, how do we break the loop? We look at what worked.

- **DARPA:** Give expert managers the power to make weird, counterintuitive bets without ruining their careers if they fail.
    
- **Bell Labs:** Hire the smartest people, give them resources, and force them to interact.
    
- **Operation Warp Speed (OWS):** Act like a venture capitalist. Spread the bets, and use government muscle to smash through regulatory walls.
    

OWS proved the power of singular focus. It requires being a "bottleneck detective"—finding the specific choke point in an industry and clearing it. It means using tools like "advance market commitments" to guarantee a buyer for a new invention, pulling innovation into the present.

Ultimately, this is a political choice. Crises like Sputnik or a pandemic force focus. But we don't have to wait for the sky to fall. Leaders can choose to treat housing and climate like the emergencies they are. This "liberalism that builds" challenges the zero-sum politics of scarcity. It requires dismantling the very labyrinths we built to stop bad things from happening, so that we can finally start making good things happen. It swaps a laundry list of policies for a single, clarifying lens: What do we need to be abundant? And who is standing in the way?
