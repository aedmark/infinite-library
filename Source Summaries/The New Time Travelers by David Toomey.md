## The Chronological blues: A Look at _The New Time Travelers_

David Toomey’s _The New Time Travelers_ feels less like a dry physics ledger and more like eavesdropping on a conversation that’s been running for a century. It starts, as most good things do, with a bit of fiction. specifically, H.G. Wells and his Victorian parlor trick in _The Time Machine_. You know the scene: the gaslight, the velvet chairs, the mysterious model vanishing into thin air.

Wells nailed the _vibe_—the roller coaster terror of hurling yourself through the fourth dimension—but he pulled a fast one on the mechanics. He never explained _how_ the thing worked. He left that blank space for a twelve-year-old David Toomey to stare at, dissatisfied and hungry for answers.

But here’s the kicker: that parlor conversation didn’t stop when Wells put down his pen. It migrated from fiction into the very real, very terrifying chalkboards of theoretical physics. We’re talking about serious people—astrophysicists, mathematicians—gathering in rooms that smell like coffee and anxiety, using relativity and quantum mechanics to try and fill in the blanks Wells left behind.

### The Moment the Laughter Stopped

For decades, talking about time travel in a physics department was a great way to kill your career. It was the "crazy uncle" of science—fun at parties, but you wouldn't let him manage your portfolio. You wrote papers on "causality violation" because saying "time machine" meant you weren't serious.

Then came 1988.

Kip Thorne, Mike Morris, and Ulvi Yurtsever dropped a paper titled "Wormholes, Time Machines and the Weak Energy Condition" in _Physical Review Letters_. It was a quiet earthquake. They weren't saying, "Hey, let's build a DeLorean." They were saying, "Under the laws of general relativity, as we understand them, building a pathway to the past is not technically impossible."

That was the permission slip everyone needed. Suddenly, the stigma evaporated. You had heavy hitters like Stephen Hawking and Frank Tipler arguing about closed timelike curves at workshops in Aspen. Science fiction and "hard" science started trading notes. Thorne even did his work on wormholes because Carl Sagan needed a realistic way to move characters around for his novel _Contact_. The barrier between "imagined" and "real" turned out to be porous.

### So, How Do We Actually Leave?

Toomey breaks down the menu of options, and honestly, some of them are terrifying.

**The One-Way Ticket (Futureward)**

Going forward is actually the easy part. Einstein sorted this out ages ago. It’s all about **Time Dilation**.

If you move fast enough—like, really fast, edging up on the speed of light—time slows down for you relative to the people you left behind. Imagine an astronaut, let's call him Gassendi, blasting off to a star 36 light-years away. He creates a loop and comes back. For him? Maybe a few years have passed. He’s arguably still young. But on Earth? Seventy years have gone by. Everyone he knew is old or dead. He has traveled to the future, but the cost was his entire life's context.

You can do the same thing by hanging out near a supermassive black hole. Gravity warps time just like speed does. If you can survive the tidal forces (which, granted, usually turn you into spaghetti), you could hover near the event horizon and watch the universe age in fast-forward.

**The Impossible Return (Pastward)**

Going back is where the headaches start. This requires twisting the universe into shapes it really doesn't want to be in.

- **The Cylinders:** Back in the 30s, Willem Van Stockum figured out that an infinitely long cylinder of spinning dust could drag spacetime around with it, creating a loop back to the past. The catch? It has to be _infinite_. And we don't have any infinite cylinders lying around.
    
- **Wormholes:** This is the darling of modern theory.
    

![Image of traversable wormhole diagram](https://encrypted-tbn0.gstatic.com/licensed-image?q=tbn:ANd9GcSDfTF9yWKlQaY228m6xcIJ-EJF3PR_MNuFp2PW39Il-r-m8NxgV-Y6tAyzWS6OcR_IBfQ6bMox7rqYNyuOc8n2YADQIW_f_SuLJ1BajH6qHzo7Cto)

A wormhole is a tunnel connecting two points in spacetime. If you take one end of the tunnel, accelerate it to near light speed (aging it slower), and then bring it back, you have two mouths existed in different times. Step in one, step out yesterday. The problem? You need "negative energy" to keep the throat open, or gravity slams it shut. And negative energy is... elusive.

- **Cosmic Strings:** J. Richard Gott proposed using these hypothetical, ultra-dense threads left over from the Big Bang.
    

![Image of cosmic strings warping spacetime](https://encrypted-tbn3.gstatic.com/licensed-image?q=tbn:ANd9GcTO2cPEC_6Q64rvzTna9z-ydddpF8Id3rb23RxMSGMLEkP4QtKJxQJJzsEg-VQzQSunlt33LIqz2SYvMY2eEYrrEDd1Wvsy1cmFmiMMnchcgHqsRBg)

If two of them whip past each other, they might warp space enough to let you fly around them and arrive before you left.

### The Headache of Causality

The universe seems to hate a paradox. The moment you talk about going back, you run into the **Grandfather Paradox**. You know the drill: you go back, stop your grandfather from meeting your grandmother, you are never born, so you can't go back, so you _are_ born. It’s a logical short-circuit.

Physicists like Igor Novikov proposed the **Self-Consistency Principle** to stop us from losing our minds. It suggests the universe essentially edits itself. You try to shoot your grandfather? The gun jams. You try to push him off a cliff? You slip. The timeline is a stubborn thing; it bends, but it won’t break.

Then there’s the **Bootstrap Paradox**, or the "Jinn." This is the spookiest one. Imagine I copy a book from the future, go back in time, and give it to the author before they write it. They publish it. I buy it in the future to copy it. _Who wrote the book?_ The information exists without an origin. It’s an object without a creator.

### The "Many Worlds" Escape Hatch

Some physicists, like David Deutsch, look at these paradoxes and shrug. They lean into the **Multiverse**.

In this view, if you go back and prevent your birth, you haven't erased yourself. You’ve just created (or moved to) a _new_ branch of reality. In Timeline A, you were born and left. In Timeline B, you weren't born. Both exist. You aren't changing the past; you're just visiting a different neighborhood of the quantum multiverse.

### The Great Silence

If all this is possible, where are the tourists? Why isn't this article being interrupted by a historian from the year 4000?

Hawking suggested the **Chronology Protection Conjecture**—basically, the universe has a "Do Not Disturb" sign, and the laws of physics will always conspire to prevent time travel to keep history safe. Or maybe we’re just boring. Or maybe, sadly, the technology only works backward to the point it was switched on, and nobody has plugged one in yet.

Toomey’s book leaves us with a heavy, beautiful thought. We are scratching at the door of the impossible, driven by the same thing that drove H.G. Wells: a refusal to accept that time is a one-way street, and a desperate hope that maybe, just maybe, we can go back and fix things.
