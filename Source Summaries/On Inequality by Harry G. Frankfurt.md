**Stop Counting Other People's Money: Why Equality is the Wrong Goal**

Harry Frankfurt is the guy at the dinner party who waits until everyone is furiously agreeing on something, and then gently pulls the tablecloth out from under the china. In _On Inequality_, he tackles one of the sacred cows of modern discourse: the idea that the gap between the rich and the poor is, in itself, a moral abomination.

Frankfurt says: No. It’s not.

If that makes your blood pressure spike, hang on. He isn’t saying poverty is fine. He’s saying that our obsession with the _gap_—the mathematical difference between my bank account and Jeff Bezos’s—is a distraction. The moral tragedy isn't that some people have a super-yacht; it’s that other people are starving. If everyone had a safe home, good food, and a fulfilling life, would it honestly matter if someone else had a golden toilet?

**The Doctrine of Sufficiency**

Frankfurt proposes we swap out "Equality" for "Sufficiency."

The goal shouldn't be for everyone to have the _same_, but for everyone to have _enough_. And he doesn’t mean "enough to not die." He means enough to be content. To reach a state where a lack of money isn't the reason you’re unhappy.

It’s a fascinating mental shift. If you have "enough," you might still enjoy finding a twenty-dollar bill on the sidewalk, but you aren't laying awake at night scheming for more. You aren't structurally anxious. Contentment, in Frankfurt’s view, isn't about being a monk who rejects the world; it’s about reaching a point where looking at your own life feels more important than looking at your neighbor’s.

**Why the "Equality" Obsession is Toxic**

Frankfurt argues that Egalitarianism (the pursuit of equality for its own sake) is actually kind of dangerous.

1. **It’s a Red Herring:** It fixes our eyes on the distribution charts rather than the suffering of actual human beings. It creates a world where we might try to pull the rich down just to narrow the gap, without actually lifting the poor up.
    
2. **The Comparison Trap:** This is the psychological killer. Focusing on equality forces you to define your well-being by comparing yourself to others. It’s alienating. It hollows you out. Instead of asking, "Do I have what I need to be happy?", you ask, "Do I have as much as _them_?" That path leads to a shallow, envious existence.
    
3. **The "Respect" Mix-up:** We often confuse treating people _equally_ with treating them with _respect_. They aren't the same. If you know nothing about a group of people, dividing a cake equally is the respectful move because you have no other data. But if you know one person is starving and the other is full, giving them equal slices is actually _disrespectful_ because it ignores their reality. Respect means looking at the specific person and their specific needs. Equality is just a blind algorithm.
    

**The Cold Logic of Survival**

Frankfurt also takes a sledgehammer to the economic argument of "diminishing marginal utility"—the idea that a dollar is always worth less to a rich person than a poor one. He points out that sometimes, you need a critical mass of money to unlock a benefit (like saving up for a house or a specialized medical treatment). Taking that money away to distribute it in tiny, equal slivers might mean _nobody_ gets the house or the cure.

He uses a brutal example: If there is enough medicine to keep 5 people alive, but we have 10 sick patients, Egalitarianism might suggest giving everyone a half-dose. The result? Everyone dies. The Morality of Sufficiency says you save the 5 you can, and you weep for the others, but you do not sacrifice life on the altar of fairness.
