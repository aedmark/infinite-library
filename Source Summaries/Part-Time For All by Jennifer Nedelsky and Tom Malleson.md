## The "Burnout is a Policy Failure" Manifesto

If you’ve ever felt like your life is just a series of frantic sprints between your job and your laundry pile, Jennifer Nedelsky and Tom Malleson have a message for you: **You aren't failing. The system is.**

In _Part-Time for All_, they stop trying to "lean in" or "hack" work-life balance. Instead, they propose we take the current social contract, rip it in half, and tape it back together with "Care" in the center. It is a radical, terrifying, and deeply alluring proposal to cure the "sad pathology of workaholism."

#### The Diagnosis: Why We Are All So Tired

The authors argue we are suffering from four self-inflicted wounds:

1. **Families are Breaking:** We force people to choose between feeding their kids (career) and actually raising them (time).
    
2. **Care is for "Losers":** Society treats care work—cleaning, nursing, parenting—as low-status drudgery, usually dumped on women.1
    
3. **The Bosses are Clueless:** The people making our laws usually have "care-wives" or hired help. They have literally no idea how much work it takes to keep a human alive, so they write laws that ignore it.
    
4. **Time Poverty:** We are starving for minutes.
    

#### The Fix: The 30/22 Rule

Here is the headline proposal. It’s simple, but it changes everything.

- **The Ceiling:** No one—not the CEO, not the lawyer, not the factory worker—works for pay more than **30 hours a week**.2
    
- **The Floor:** Everyone—yes, even the CEO—must do roughly **20-25 hours of unpaid care work** a week.
    

The logic? If the high-powered lawyer has to leave at 2 p.m. to care for their elderly neighbor or pick up kids, two things happen. First, they stop looking down on people who do care work because they _are_ people who do care work. Second, they stop writing laws that make care impossible.

#### Re-Branding "Dependence"

We love the myth of the "Self-Made Man." Nedelsky and Malleson call this a dangerous fantasy. Nobody is self-made. We are all "dependents" at the beginning and end of our lives, and likely somewhere in the middle too.

By forcing everyone to participate in care, we destroy the stigma. Care isn't a penalty box for those who couldn't "make it" in the market; it’s the rent we pay for living in a community. And it doesn't have to be just diapers and dishes. It’s about **"Communities of Care."** If you don't have kids or elderly parents, you don't get a pass. You support a friend, you join a care circle, you help a neighbor. You get your hands dirty with the messy business of being human.

#### But... How? (The Logistics)

I know what you're thinking. "My boss would laugh in my face." Or, "I can't afford to work 30 hours."

The authors aren't naive. They admit this requires a massive overhaul:

- **Job Sharing:** Two people sharing one "intense" job.
    
- **The Safety Net:** This **cannot** work if it privatizes care. It requires robust social democracy—universal healthcare, childcare, and housing security. You can't ask people to work less if it means they starve.
    
- **The Cultural Shift:** We have to stop asking "What do you do?" at parties and judging the answer by the paycheck size.
    

It’s not a Utopia. There will still be stress. There will still be dirty diapers. But it swaps the stress of "survival" for the stress of "connection."
