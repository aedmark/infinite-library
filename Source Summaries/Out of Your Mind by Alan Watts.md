## The Cosmic Game of Hide-and-Seek

Alan Watts has a way of looking at the human condition that feels like someone finally finding the light switch in a dark, cluttered room. In "Out of Your Mind," he takes a sledgehammer to the way we usually think about reality, and honestly, it’s about time. We’ve been walking around with some pretty rusty mental maps, trying to navigate a territory that is far wilder and weirder than we give it credit for.

Watts points out that most of us are operating on one of two "myths" about the universe. The first is the **Ceramic Model**. This is the old-school idea that the universe was made by a King-God, like a potter making a pot. In this version, you are an artifact. You were "made," you are separate from your Maker, and you are essentially a subject in a cosmic monarchy. It’s a bit stiff, isn't it? It feels like being a piece of furniture in a museum where you aren't allowed to touch anything.

When people got tired of that, they swung to the other extreme: the **Fully Automatic Model**. This is the grim, scientific materialism view. Here, the universe is a blind, stupid machine. It’s just energy buzzing around with no point, and you are a biological fluke—a little flash of consciousness trapped in a dying meat-suit, fighting a hostile environment. It’s lonely. It’s alienating. It makes you feel like a stranger in your own house.

### You Are The Big Bang

Watts offers a third option, and it’s a breath of fresh air: The **Dramatic Model**.

He suggests that we stop thinking of ourselves as things that came _into_ this world, and start realizing we came _out_ of it. Think of an apple tree. When it apples, you don't say the apples are strangers to the tree. They are the tree doing its thing. In the same way, the universe "peoples." You are a symptom of the cosmos. You are not a result of the Big Bang; you are the Big Bang, still banging, right here and now.

This shifts the whole picture. If you are the works—the whole show—then life isn't a test (Ceramic) and it isn't a tragedy (Automatic). It’s a game. It’s a drama. It’s the ultimate game of Hide-and-Seek.

Imagine you are the ultimate reality—God, the Brahman, the Tao, whatever label you like. If you have infinite time and infinite power, eventually, you’d get bored. You’d want a surprise. You’d want to get lost just for the thrill of being found. So, you pretend to be _you_. You pretend to be a stressed-out accountant, or a nervous philosopher, or a person reading this article. You pretend so well that you forget you’re pretending. That’s the "hide." The spiritual journey? That’s just the "seek."

### The Net of Jewels

This leads to a beautiful, dizzying idea: the world is a "net of jewels." Every individual is like a dewdrop on a spiderweb, and every dewdrop reflects every other dewdrop. You can’t pluck one part out without the whole thing changing. This is what Watts calls "mutual interpenetration."

We spend so much energy defending our "skin-encapsulated ego"—that feeling of being a "me" inside a bag of skin—but that boundary is an illusion. Your inside goes with your outside. You can’t have a front without a back. You can’t have a solid without space. You can’t have "self" without "other." They aren't enemies; they are dance partners.

When you really metabolize this, the fear of death starts to dissolve. We are terrified of the "void," of the blankness. But Watts reminds us that the void isn't nothing; it’s the container for everything. You need the silence between the notes to make music. If sound never stopped, it would just be a deafening screech. The "off" is just as important as the "on."

### Shaking the Glue

The problem is, we get "sticky." We get hung up (what the Buddhists call _klesha_) on trying to make the good moments last forever and trying to push the bad moments away. We try to "control" the flow. But have you ever tried to smooth rough water with a flatiron? You just make more waves.

The secret, the great punchline of Zen, is that there is nothing you need to _do_ to become this universal self. You already are it. Trying to "become" enlightened is like trying to bite your own teeth. It’s already happening. Your heart is beating, your breath is flowing, your cells are doing a million complex things right now without you micromanaging them. That is the universe working through you.

So, the invitation is to stop taking the drama so seriously. Realize you’re the actor in the mask. Wiggle a little. Accept the "quaking mess" of being human—the fear, the joy, the confusion—because that quake is the vibration of the cosmos itself. You aren't a victim of the play; you wrote the script, and you’re playing the lead, just for the fun of it.
