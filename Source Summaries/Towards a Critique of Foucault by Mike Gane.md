Let’s talk about Michel Foucault. If philosophy had rock stars, he’d be the one wearing a leather jacket, smoking a cigarette, and telling you that your concept of "sanity" is just a cage built by the 17th century.

The book in question, _Towards a Critique of Foucault_ (edited by Mike Gane), isn't just a fan letter. It’s a collection of essays from the journal _Economy and Society_, written back when Foucault was still alive and kicking (or just shortly after he left us). It captures a moment in time when thinkers—specifically those leaning toward Marxism and social theory—were trying to figure out what to do with this French powerhouse. Was he a friend? A foe? Or something entirely new that messed up everyone’s filing system?

### The Man Who Studied Everything

Foucault didn’t stay in a lane. The guy went from studying madness to hospitals, then to prisons, and finally to sexuality. To an outsider, it looks like a random grab bag of "edgy" topics. But Foucault saw them all as the same project. He wasn't just looking at _things_; he was looking at the invisible wires that connect knowledge to power.

He didn't just ask, "How did we treat crazy people in 1650?" He asked, "How did we invent the _idea_ of mental illness so we could build a wall around 'reason'?" It’s a subtle difference, but it changes everything. He treated these things—medicine, law, psychiatry—not as sciences getting better at finding the truth, but as tools building specific kinds of people.

### digging vs. Tracing (The Method)

Foucault shifted his toolkit over the years.

- **Archaeology:** In his earlier stuff (like _The Order of Things_), he acted like an excavator. He wanted to uncover the hidden rules that allowed people to think certain thoughts in a specific era.
    
- **Genealogy:** Later (inspired by Nietzsche), he switched to "Genealogy." This wasn't about digging for static layers; it was about tracing the messy, often violent family tree of concepts like "justice" or "sexuality."
    

He was fiercely "anti-humanist." Now, don't panic—that doesn't mean he hated humans. It means he didn't believe in the "Sovereign Individual"—the idea that _you_ are the sole author of your life, making rational choices in a vacuum. Foucault argued that history isn't made by Great Men making Great Choices; history is a texture of forces that _creates_ the subject. You don't speak language; language speaks you.

### The Marxist Beef

Here is where the essays in the book get spicy. In the 80s, Marxists were struggling with Foucault.

- **The Good:** Some saw him as a bridge. He was talking about oppression and power, which Marxists love.
    
- **The Bad:** Peter Dews and Perry Anderson (heavy hitters in this collection) were less impressed. Anderson basically accused Foucault of being a "technocratic functionalist" and later a "neo-anarchist." The main gripe? Foucault didn't care about _causation_.
    

Foucault was great at saying, "Hey, in 1789, the way we punished people changed completely."

Historians would ask, "Why?"

And Foucault would essentially shrug and say, "The discursive formation shifted."

For a Marxist who believes in economic causes for everything, this is infuriating. They accused him of celebrating chance and ignoring the "why" in favor of the "how."

### The Power-Knowledge Loop (Pouvoir-Savoir)

If you take one thing from this critique, let it be the headache of _Pouvoir-Savoir_ (Power-Knowledge). Foucault argued you can't have one without the other.

- **Old View:** Knowledge is pure (science), and Power is dirty (politics). Power tries to corrupt Knowledge.
    
- **Foucault's View:** Power _creates_ fields of knowledge. The prison system created "criminology." The asylum created "psychiatry." And that knowledge, in turn, reinforces the power.
    

Michael Donnelly’s essay in the collection points out that while this is brilliant, Foucault struggles to explain how this actually works in the modern day without sounding like a conspiracy theorist. He talks about a "Disciplinary Society" where we are all being watched and measured, but he’s vague on _who_ is doing the watching and _why_ it’s coordinated so well if there's no central "Subject" running the show.

### Where is the Escape Hatch?

The biggest anxiety in these essays is about Resistance.

If power is everywhere—if it’s in our language, our desires, and our very identity—how do you fight it?

Foucault talked about the "plebs"—not a specific class of poor people, but a sort of "centrifugal energy" that resists power. But his critics, like Gary Wickham, found this weak. If resistance is just part of the power game (a reaction to every action), then true revolution seems impossible. It implies that every time we try to overthrow the system, we’re just participating in it.

### The Takeaway

This collection portrays Foucault not as a god of philosophy, but as a brilliant, frustrating toolbox. He shattered the old ways of doing history, but he left a lot of broken glass on the floor. He forces us to look at how we are "subjectified"—how we are turned into "subjects" (identities like 'student', 'criminal', 'voter')—by the very institutions meant to help us.
