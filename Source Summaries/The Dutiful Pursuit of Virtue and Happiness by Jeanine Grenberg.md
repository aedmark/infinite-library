## The Great Philosophy Mash-Up: How to Be Good Without Being Miserable

We usually think of Immanuel Kant as the strict school principal of philosophy. He’s the guy wagging a finger, telling you to do your duty not because it feels good, but because _it’s the rules_. On the other side of the hallway, we have Aristotle and the "happiness" crew, telling you that the whole point of life is to flourish and feel amazing about it.

Jeanine Grenberg’s _The Dutiful Pursuit of Virtue and Happiness_ proposes something radical: The Principal and the Party Animal are actually talking about the same thing. She calls it **Deontological Eudaemonism**.

This is a mouthful, but the concept is beautiful. It argues that for complex creatures like us—who have big brains (reason) but also hungry bellies and hearts (sensibility)—true happiness isn't just getting what we want. It’s getting what we want _while_ governed by a stable, necessary principle. That principle? **Respect for Persons**.

### The Objective Telos: The North Star

Imagine your desires are a rowdy room full of toddlers. If you let them rule the house ("happiness empirically conceived"), you get chaos. You get ice cream for breakfast and a house on fire. You need a grown-up in the room.

That grown-up is the **Objective Telos**. This is the goal that organizes the chaos. It isn't just a stop sign saying "Don't hit your sister." It is a positive command: _Treat humanity as an end in itself._ Every goal you set—whether it's learning the cello or traveling to Italy—must be filtered through this lens. You aren't just traveling; you are traveling in a way that respects the absolute value of the people you meet and the person you are.

### The Subjective Telos: The Kantian "Flow State"

Here is where it gets really interesting, and where the text brings in a surprise guest: **Taoism**.

Usually, we think doing "duty" is hard work. It’s grinding your teeth and doing the right thing. But Grenberg argues for a **Subjective Telos**—an internal state of "harmony, facility, and ease."

When you become truly virtuous, doing the right thing doesn't feel like a struggle against your desires. It feels like a **free aptitude**. It flows. This is strikingly similar to the Taoist concept of _Wu Wei_ (non-action or effortless action). A virtuous person doesn't have to white-knuckle their way through morality. They have cultivated a "moral apathy" to the petty stuff and a "moral excitement" for the good stuff.

They have practiced virtue so much that it becomes their natural state. They aren't forcing the door open; they are walking through an open archway.

### The Humanist Web: May, Scanlon, and You

But wait, isn't this all a bit lonely? Just you and your duty?

This is where the synthesis with **Todd May** and **T.M. Scanlon** adds the final layer of warmth. May argues for "Relational Autonomy"—the idea that we only become fully ourselves _through_ other people. You can't be a virtuous island.

Scanlon adds the "Reason-Giving" principle: You should act in a way that you could justify to the people affected by your actions.

When you combine all of this—Grenberg’s "Respect," Taoism’s "Flow," and Humanism’s "Connection"—you get a powerful vision of life. You pursue your hobbies and loves (empirical happiness), but you do it with a skillfulness (virtue) that protects and honors the web of relationships you live in. You don't just "follow the rules." You navigate the world with a kind of moral grace, ensuring that your flourishing helps everyone else flourish, too.
