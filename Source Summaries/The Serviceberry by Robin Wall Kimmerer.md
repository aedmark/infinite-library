It begins with a stain on the tongue and a startling realization: this didn't cost a dime. You find yourself standing before a Serviceberry bush—or Shadbush, or Juneberry, depending on who taught you the names of things—and you are eating. The sweetness isn't earned. It isn't a return on investment. It is a handout from the earth.

In our current operating system, this feels almost illicit. We are trained to reach for our wallets before we reach for the fruit. But here, the transaction is absent. There is only the bush, the berry, and the mouth. This is the first lesson of the Serviceberry: gratitude is the only logical response to a world that sustains you without sending an invoice.

Robin Wall Kimmerer notes that for the Potawatomi, this concept is woven into the linguistics. The word _min_ denotes a berry, but it functions as a synonym for "gift." It reframes the landscape not as a warehouse of natural resources waiting to be extracted, but as a community of relatives offering sustenance. It is a system of abundance where the berry does not discriminate between the saint and the sinner. It simply gives.

This stands in stark, violent contrast to the artificial scarcity that drives our market economy. We have been indoctrinated with the belief that for me to have more, you must have less. This is the mathematics of separation, a lonely calculus that reduces neighbors to competitors and the land to real estate. The Anishinaabe tradition personifies this sickness as the Windigo—a legendary monster condemned to an insatiable hunger. The more the Windigo consumes, the more it starves. It is the embodiment of a capitalistic feedback loop: consumption without satisfaction, hoarding without security.

A rigid economist might scoff, straightening his tie to explain that the bush is simply maximizing its self-interest. It’s just biological capitalism, surely? But biology disagrees. We are learning that the "survival of the fittest" narrative was always a clumsy oversimplification. Evolution is often driven by cooperation. The Serviceberry does not hoard its sugars; it feasts the birds. In return, the birds carry the seeds to new soil. If the bush attempted to be a rugged individualist, hoarding its energy and refusing to share, it would perish. Flourishing is a mutual act.

In this economy, the currency is relationship. When you receive a gift, the bond does not end; it begins. You do not "pay it back" to close the ledger; you "pay it forward" to keep the energy moving. It is a circle, not a line. We know this feeling—we are born into it. Every infant enters a maternal gift economy, where survival depends entirely on generosity that seeks no immediate financial return. We merely need to remember that the earth holds us in that same embrace.

This is not a utopian fantasy for a bygone era. It is a practical application for the here and now. We see it in the quiet radicalism of a neighbor who invites strangers to pick fruit from their tree, trading potential profit for social glue. We see it in the public library, a sanctuary where knowledge is shared rather than sold, a commons that defies the logic of the marketplace.

The shift required is internal. We must evict the "Rational Economic Man" from our heads—that lonely hoarder counting beans in the dark—and invite in the participant, the neighbor, the sharer. We must decide that the sound of crickets and the taste of wild berries are better metrics for a good life than the numbers on a quarterly report.
