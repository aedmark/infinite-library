**The Pregnant Pause: Rebecca Hill and the Space Between**

We need to talk about the gap. You know, the empty space between two cars at a stoplight, the awkward silence in a conversation, the "half-time" at a football game. In English, and in French (_l’intervalle_), we usually treat the **interval** as a dead zone. It’s a nothingness that we measure to keep things apart. It is a vacuum waiting to be filled.

Rebecca Hill, in her book _The Interval_, looks at that empty space and shakes her head. She argues that we have it all wrong. The interval isn't empty; it is _heavy_. It isn't a passive gap; it is a **generative threshold**. It is the active, vibrating volcanic soil from which identity, difference, and everything else grows. It is the noise between the notes that makes the music possible.

**The Ghost in the Foundation: Irigaray’s Critique**

Hill draws her battle lines using the work of Luce Irigaray. Irigaray’s main beef with Western philosophy is that it is built on a "phallocentric" model.1 That’s a fancy way of saying that for thousands of years, male philosophers have built systems that look a lot like themselves: solid, singular, and upright.

In this architectural project, the "Maternal-Feminine" got shoved into the basement. Woman became the passive container—the vessel—for the male subject. But Hill points out that Irigaray isn’t just complaining about who gets to speak. She is obsessed with the **interval** between the philosopher and the mother he repressed. That repressed relationship is the secret mortar holding the whole building together.

Irigaray argues that if we want to actually think about _difference_—specifically sexual difference—we have to stop looking at the two people and start looking at the space between them. This interval is an "open threshold." It’s not about fusion (becoming one blob). It’s about two distinct forces vibrating against each other, creating a dynamic tension that allows new things to emerge.

**The Old Guard: Aristotle and Bergson**

Hill drags two heavyweights into the ring to prove her point: Aristotle and Henri Bergson.2

Aristotle: The Box Maker

Aristotle loved categories. He defined "place" (topos) essentially as a vessel—a container that holds a thing. Hill argues that Aristotle accidentally admits that "place" relies on a relationship between bodies, but he panicked and tried to bolt it down. He rejected the diastēma (the interval) because it was too messy, too mobile. He wanted a fixed address for everything. Irigaray flips this. She says, "Yes! The interval is mobile! It’s porous!" She takes the very thing Aristotle threw in the trash—the mobile, uncontainable gap—and makes it the hero of the story.

**Bergson: The Time Keeper**

Then there is Bergson. He was obsessed with **duration** (_la durée_). He hated how we try to slice time up into static moments (like seconds on a clock). For him, real time is a flow, a melting, an intuition. Hill likes that he sees the interval as a threshold for the new, but she catches him in a trap. Bergson uses a lot of "paternal" metaphors—height, erection, purity—to describe time, while dumping on "space" and "matter" as being passive and feminine.3 He tries to separate time from the body, but Hill argues you can’t have one without the other.

**The Sticky Truth: Mucus and Placentas**

Here is where it gets visceral. To explain what a "generative interval" actually looks like, Hill and Irigaray stop using abstract shapes and start looking at biology.

Think about the **placenta**.

It is a wild organ. It connects the mother and the fetus, but it also keeps them separate. It negotiates the exchange of blood and nutrients without the two beings fusing into one identity. It is a distinct threshold that allows for a relationship based on difference. It implies a future.

Or consider **mucus**. Yes, really. Mucus is the ultimate interval. It expands, it connects, it lubricates the passage between bodies, yet it resists being measured or counted. You can’t stack mucus. It is "in subtraction from the order of number." It is the physical proof that the space between us isn't empty air; it is a medium of connection.

**Becoming vs. Relation**

Ultimately, Hill concludes that the interval does two jobs. It is **Relation** (the connection between subjects, like the spark between lovers) and it is **Becoming** (the raw force of change itself). It is the realization that we are never fully "finished" products. We are always in the process of being made and unmade by the spaces we inhabit and the people we touch. The interval is the promise that there is always something left over, something more to come.
