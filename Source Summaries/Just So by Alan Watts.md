## The Great Cosmic Bumper Cars

Alan Watts had a knack for pulling the rug out from under the Western mind, and in “Just So,” he goes straight for the foundation: our language. We think we see the world as it is, but really, we’re looking at it through a pair of grammatic spectacles we forgot we were wearing.

In the West, we’re obsessed with the "billiard ball" model of reality. We treat the universe like a pool table where separate, lonely little particles bounce off each other. This is an old hangover from Democritus and the Greeks, who gave us the word _atom_ (literally "uncuttable"). Even though we split the atom a long time ago, we still act like the world is made of Lego bricks—discrete, disconnectable chunks. You are a chunk, I am a chunk, and that tree over there is a chunk.

But Watts says this model is bust. It’s bad physics and even worse philosophy.

### The Ocean Doesn't "Have" Waves

Shift the metaphor. Instead of billiard balls, think of the ocean. You can point to a wave and say, "Look at that wave," but you know you can't cut the wave out of the ocean with a knife. The wave _is_ the ocean waving.

This is where the Western brain usually short-circuits. We are taught that we came _into_ this world. Watts corrects the preposition: We came _out_ of it. You are a symptom of the universe. You are an aperture through which the cosmos is observing itself. We aren't separate entities walking around on a dead rock; we are the rock, peopling.

This changes the whole game of "opposites." You can’t have a front without a back. You can’t have a solid without space. You can’t have a buyer without a seller. They aren't fighting each other; they are generating each other. It’s a vibratory dance. Existence is an on/off switch, and you need both positions to have a current.

### The Celestial Manager is Out to Lunch

Our other big hang-up is the idea of "Natural Law." This is pure Judeo-Christian theology dressed up in a lab coat. We view the universe as a monarchy—a kingdom where stuff happens because a Big Boss (God, Physics, The Laws of Nature) said so. We look for the "laws" that govern gravity the same way we look for the laws that govern traffic.

Watts points out that this is a projection. We are projecting our own love of police officers and city councils onto the cosmos. In the Chinese Taoist view, nature doesn't have a boss. It has _Ziran_—"self-so." It happens of itself.

Think about your own body. You don't command your heart to beat. You don't draft a memo to your stomach to digest that sandwich. It just happens. It is self-organizing intelligence. The universe is the same way. It’s not a machine built by an engineer; it’s an organism growing itself.

### The Net of Jewels

Watts offers a dazzling image to replace the billiard table: The Net of Indra. Imagine a spiderweb covered in morning dew. Every single drop of dew contains the reflection of every other drop on the web. And in each of those reflected drops, there are reflections of all the other drops, infinitely.

This is the doctrine of mutual interpenetration. You are a dewdrop. You reflect the entire universe, and the entire universe requires you to be exactly where you are to complete the picture. If you remove one mesh from a net, the whole thing unravels. You aren't a lonely puppet; you are a focal point for the entire grid of existence.

### The Ego Hoax

So, if we are this magnificent, self-organizing cosmic energy, why do we feel like anxious little meat-sacks trapped in a scary world?

Watts blames the "skin-encapsulated ego." We have been hypnotized into believing that "I" is a little man sitting somewhere behind the eyes and between the ears, driving the body like a truck.

This "I" is a fiction. It’s a social convention, like the equator. The equator is useful for navigation, but you can’t trip over it. The ego is useful for organizing appointments, but it doesn't actually exist as a physical entity. The anxiety comes from trying to protect this imaginary boundary, trying to make the "I" permanent in a universe that is defined by flow.

### Putting Legs on a Snake

We spend our lives trying to grasp this reality, to "get it." But Watts laughs at this. You can't "get" it because you _are_ it. Trying to catch the fundamental energy of the universe is like trying to bite your own teeth.

In Zen, they call this "putting legs on a snake"—unnecessary effort that actually ruins the thing. You don't need to try to be the universe. You are it. The only thing you can really do is "let it happen."

This isn't about becoming a passive lump. It’s about realizing that your "active" decisions and the "passive" events of nature are the same process. When you sing, or saw wood, or drive a car, and you’re really in the zone, the "I" disappears. The singing sings itself. The driving drives itself. That is the feeling of _Ziran_.

### The Wheel of "Just For Fun"

Life, then, isn't a problem to be solved. It’s not a journey to a destination, because the destination is the graveyard. It’s a musical thing. You’re supposed to dance while the music plays.

We treat life like a "rat race" or a "sorry-go-round" because we are obsessed with the future, obsessed with winning. But if you realize that the center of the wheel is everywhere, and that you are the whole works, you can flip the script. The rat race becomes a mandala—a beautiful, circular dance.

You play the game of life, but you play it like a wealthy gambler who knows the money doesn't matter. You play for the thrill of the spin. You realize that the "mystery" isn't something to be explained away by words or formulas. It’s a reality to be experienced.

As Watts puts it, the mystery of life is not a riddle to be solved, but a dance to be danced.
